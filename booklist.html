On Elinor Ostrom and Governing the Commons

Most theories of collective action begin with a grim assumption: that individuals cannot cooperate without coercion. Either a central authority must enforce rules, or privatization must eliminate shared incentives. Left to ourselves, the logic goes, we ruin what we share.

Elinor Ostrom didn’t believe that. In *Governing the Commons*, she challenges this narrative—not with abstract models but with empirical evidence. She documents how real communities manage shared resources like fisheries, irrigation systems, and pastures without top-down control. Her case studies range from Nepal to Los Angeles, from Swiss villages to Japanese forests. In each, the tragedy of the commons is not inevitable. It is a possibility, not a fate.

Ostrom’s core idea is deceptively simple: under the right conditions, people are capable of self-organization. They can create rules, monitor each other, sanction defectors, and adapt institutions over time. Her famous “design principles” summarize what makes this work—clear boundaries, graduated sanctions, collective-choice arrangements, local monitoring, nested governance. None of these require state power. What they require is trust, local knowledge, and time.

The book is dry in places. It reads like a careful academic rebuttal, not a sweeping narrative. But that restraint is part of its power. Where others proclaim, Ostrom verifies. She shows what cooperative governance looks like in practice, and why theories built on homo economicus miss the point. People are not only self-interested—they are embedded in relationships, traditions, constraints, and feedback loops. Her work maps the terrain where theory meets reality.

In a moment when climate change, digital platforms, and AI raise new questions about managing shared risks, Ostrom’s book remains startlingly relevant. It doesn’t provide a blueprint. It offers a possibility: that governance can emerge from the bottom up, if we pay attention to how real people actually solve real problems together.

On Holmes and the Path of the Law

Most legal writing tells you what the law is. Holmes’s essay tells you what the law does—and what kind of mind it takes to understand that. *The Path of the Law* is not just a foundational essay in American jurisprudence. It’s a provocation, a challenge to every reader who still thinks law is about logic, morality, or even justice in any straightforward sense.

Holmes begins with what sounds like a shrug: the law is not a brooding omnipresence in the sky, but a prediction of what courts will do. The true measure of legal knowledge is not whether you know the rule, but whether you can foresee its application. To understand the law, think like a bad man—someone who cares only about consequences, not ethics. This shift from principle to probability is the central move in Holmes’s realism.

What follows is a theory of law that is starkly pragmatic. Law is not logic, though it borrows its forms. It evolves by experience, shaped by shifting intuitions, economic interests, and political pressures. Holmes doesn’t say this to undermine the law, but to clarify its function. The job of the lawyer is not to deduce rules from first principles, but to navigate a moving landscape, where doctrine trails behind the facts on the ground.

The writing is dense but forceful. Every sentence does work. Holmes combines judicial detachment with rhetorical intensity—at times ironic, at times severe. He wants the reader to see law as a human practice, not a science, and to stop pretending otherwise. There’s no talk of natural rights, no invocation of timeless truths. Only history, habit, and hard choices.

What makes the essay last is not just its realism, but its ambition. Holmes ends with a call to “build a new legal metaphysics.” He believes law can be reimagined, but only if we first strip it of its illusions. In that sense, *The Path of the Law* is less a map than a clearing. It doesn’t tell you where to go—it tells you where the old roads end.

The Years of Lyndon Johnson and The Power Broker

There is no great policy victory without an understanding of power. That is the central lesson of Robert Caro’s work—whether it is Robert Moses reshaping New York’s physical infrastructure or Lyndon Johnson reshaping America's moral one. Caro doesn’t merely document history; he dissects the anatomy of power: how it is acquired, how it is maintained, and how it is used to bend institutions to one’s will.

Reading Caro’s biographies of Moses and Johnson, one quickly comes to see that policy success depends on the synthesis of three distinct elements:

  • Technically sound policy: It must work as intended and survive legal and bureaucratic scrutiny.
  • Narrative coherence: It must resonate with people, fitting into a broader story about what our political community is and what it is for.
  • Strategic realpolitik: It must be maneuvered through a terrain of incentives, opposition, alliances, and inertia.

What Caro reveals is that policy change is not a function of good ideas alone. It is a function of good ideas embedded in persuasive narratives, executed by power-conscious actors.
