What Robert Caro Taught Me About Power, Policy, and AI Governance

There is no great policy victory without a war for power. That is the central lesson of Robert Caro’s work—whether it is Robert Moses reshaping New York’s physical infrastructure or Lyndon Johnson reshaping its moral one. Caro doesn’t merely document history; he dissects the anatomy of power: how it is acquired, how it is maintained, and how it is used to bend institutions to one’s will.

Reading Caro’s biographies of Moses and Johnson, one quickly comes to see that policy success depends on the synthesis of three distinct elements:

  • Technically sound policy: It must work as intended and survive legal and bureaucratic scrutiny.
  • Narrative coherence: It must resonate with people, fitting into a broader story about what our political community is and what it is for.
  • Strategic realpolitik: It must be maneuvered through a terrain of incentives, opposition, alliances, and inertia.

What Caro reveals is that policy change is not a function of good ideas alone. It is a function of good ideas embedded in persuasive narratives, executed by power-conscious actors.
